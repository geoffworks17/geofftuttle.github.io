RC Plane With Needle Cutter

I was chatting with a coworker the other day, and the conversation turned to RC planes. Turns out, he was in the same place in his RC journey as I was a few months ago! We talked over the resources we had been using to build planes and the equipment we had been using, and agreed to meet up soon to do some building and flying.

This coincidence and conversation renewed my interest in flying, and got me to fire up the needle cutter again to start making planes. He recommended the FT Flyer, a good trainer plane that could be made with a single sheet of foamboard.

Cutting Parts with Ease

I have yet to find a good spoilboard that is soft enough to keep the needle from breaking during cuts, but porous enough to allow the vacuum table to hold down the foamboard.

The current solution is to place the material on a 5mm sheet of EVA foam, and hold the board down with some painter’s tape and weights at the corners. This has proven sufficient enough to hold the board for the duration of the cut, but I would like to use the vacuum to make the material available for parts from corner to corner.

Each 20" x 30" sheet takes about 30 minutes to cut. I have been using LightBurn to generate the gcode, and a python script to transform the laser intensity commands to movement of the z axis. While I could use a dedicated router CAM software to do this, the interface of Lightburn makes it is so much easier to rearrange parts for cutting.

Assembly

Once cut, the parts pop out with ease, and I assemble with hot glue, bamboo skewers, and tape. The firewall at the front of the plane is usually a lightweight plywood or balsa, but this one is 3d printed.

A 7 inch, 3-bladed propeller is attached to the brushless motor, giving it plenty of power. With a full battery, I think I can cruise at about 30% power to the throttle.

Airborne Again

For a simple design with only the elevator and rudder, I am really impressed with how well it flies. My previous planes have taught me how to build and how to crash, but this one finally taught me how to fly. It’s definitely the plane I should have started my journey with, but I suppose that is a good life lesson for keeping things simple. Check out the video below for some air action!
