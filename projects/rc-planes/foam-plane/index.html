Foam Plane

Ever since I was a kid, I have had an interest in remote controlled anything. When I was younger, I had a few RC cars, like a Rebound that could drive even when flipped over, and another dune buggy style that was less forgiving when upside down.

However, anything that flew was prohibitively expensive and probably would not have survived my “full throttle” approach to RC vehicles.

Fortunately, the rise of hobbyist electronics, DIY drones, and cheap components with cheap shipping from China has made RC planes a lot easier to get into, and a lot easier to rebuild after the inevitable crash.

There is also an amazing community out there putting out videos and free plans around this hobby. In particular, the linked video inspired me to give my first RC plane a go.

Sneak Peek and Improper Scale

As seen above, the first attempt at this was a laughable mis-judgement of scale. At the time, all I could find was the smaller version of the glider. I charged ahead though every step of assembly, growing more and more wary of the frame I had chosen to build up. Once I got to the last step of strapping on the battery, I accepted that this was not going to be a good first plane for me.

Looking back on this with fresh eyes; If I had a smaller battery, it still might have flown, but it would have been a high speed bullet and overly sensitive to any shift in the center of gravity.

Once more, but bigger!

After sourcing a properly scaled foam glider, I got to work moving the components over and rebuilding the plane. This forced rebuild was a good learning experience, and I made a few choices on the second build motivated by mistakes made on the first.

For the brushless motor, I printed a small disk with the proper mounting holes, and hot-glued the assembly into place. This was surprisingly strong, and held up under all full throttle tests.

The electronic speed controller (ESC) is probably my favorite part of this build. Using a hobby knife, I cut away the canopy and hollowed it out to make room for the electronics. I am very pleased with how clean the fit is.

For the aileron servo, I hollowed out a small cavity for a snug servo fit, and used a bit of tape and glue to hold it in place. The control horn is 3d printed and glued into place, with a paperclip acting as a control rod.

The aileron itself is a piece of foamboard taped to the edge of the wing, with a bit of a gap to allow it to move. Not the cleanest solution, but it does give a fair bit of up and down deflection when the servo moves.

At the center of the y made by the servo cables is the 4 channel receiver. To control the plane, I am using a Flysky i6 transmitter that came with a drone I picked up a few months ago.

The location of the battery made me a bit nervous, but this was the only location that would allow a proper center of gravity. I would have liked to hollow out the frame for a secure mounting spot, but I was running out of spare foam on the fuselage to carve away.

The local RC Flying club was kind enough to let me use their field for a test flight. At takeoff, the wheels provided little more than a lift to keep the prop from hitting the ground. I powered the length of the runway at about 6 feet off the ground that ended with a soft crash in some tall grass.

For the next flight, someone did a hand launch while I took the transmitter. This got much higher off the ground, but I found I had very little control over the aircraft. The plane climbed a bit higher before taking a nosedive beyond the treeline, where I found the picture from above.

If I were to build this again, I would probably put the control surfaces on the elevator and added a rudder. The surfaces all act on the center of gravity, and having things on the tail would have allowed the force to be applied to a longer arm. The ill fated setup had the control way to close to the center of gravity.

Fortunately, I had so much fun building this plane I didn’t mind the crash that much. I packed up the broken remains of the plane to extract the electronics for the next build.
