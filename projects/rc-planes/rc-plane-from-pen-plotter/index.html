RC Plane From Pen Plotter

After a bit of mulling over the last RC plane adventure, I decided to jump back in with another build. I wanted something that looked sharp, was somewhat compact, and dedicated to training.

I decided on the FT Tiny Trainer, a scratch-build design from Flite Test.

Pen Plotter

Instead of going through the hassle of printing out the design on standard sheets of printer paper, tiling them together with tape, transferring them to foamboard, and cutting them out, I wanted to use the MPCNC as a plotter to draw directly on the board.

To start, I downloaded the full size pdf plans and trimmed out the logos and pictures using Inkscape. These plans were exported as SVGs into LightBurn, my go-to laser cutting software.

Machine Setup

In Lightburn, I scaled up to the proper size, set each color to a different cut job, and exported each job as a separate gcode file. Using a python script, I transformed each “laser on” and “laser off” command to a “lower z axis” and “raise z axis.

To transfer the patterns, I attached black, blue, and red pens to the z axis. Black is for cuts, red is for scoring, and blue is for notes and alignment. I then ran each job for each pen, making sure the machine started each color at the same point.

This was so much easier than trying to transfer from tiled sheets. I suppose if I did not have the MPCNC, I would have to, or purchase one of Flite Test’s reasonably priced speedbuild kits, but I like to work with the tools I have.

With both sheets of the plan transferred, it was time to start cutting. Using an xacto knife, I cut along the black lines to remove each piece from the board, and scored along the red lines. I am glad to have the blue marks as notes during assembly, but these were not necessary.

The plane gives the option for two wings, one with polyhedral and one flat with elevons. Polyhedral aids the plane in self-righting when the aircraft rolls to the left or right. Elevons are connected to servos and move in opposite directions, giving control of aircraft roll to the pilot (or TX remote holder). I opted for the polyhedral wing to make this easier to fly.

I like how straightforward this design is, and look forward to getting it completed and in the air. Using the MPCNC to focus on the build instead the details of transferring makes the process much more enjoyable. Once I get the electronics done, it should be ready to fly.
